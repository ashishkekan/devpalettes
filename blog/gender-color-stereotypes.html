Inclusive Design

Gender & Color Stereotypes

Why the "Pink vs Blue" paradigm is outdated.

For decades, product design has been segregated by color: pink for girls, blue for boys. This binary approach is rooted in marketing strategies from the mid-20th century, not biological preference. As society moves towards a more inclusive understanding of gender, designers have the opportunity—and responsibility—to break these stereotypes.

The History of Pink and Blue

Surprisingly, before the 1940s, pink was often recommended for boys (as a stronger color) and blue for girls (as a delicate color). The flip occurred due to marketing shifts. Today, the association is so strong that it limits consumer choice. Men may avoid products they need because they are pink; women may be patronized with "shrink it and pink it" product versions.

Designing for Everyone

Gender-neutral design is on the rise. It focuses on function and aesthetic preference rather than gender. Colors like yellow, green, and purple are widely viewed as gender-neutral. Grayscale and monochromatic schemes also sidestep the issue entirely. By offering a wider palette, you allow users to self-select based on personality, not biology.

Conclusion

Color preferences are individual, not gendered. By moving beyond the pink/blue binary, we create products that respect the diversity of the human experience.

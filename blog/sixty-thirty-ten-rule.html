Design Theory

The 60-30-10 Rule

A timeless formula for perfectly balanced color schemes.

The 60-30-10 rule is a classic design principle used to create balanced and visually pleasing color schemes. Originating in interior design, it has been adopted by UI/UX designers as a reliable framework for allocating color dominance. The rule suggests a proportional distribution of three colors to create harmony, contrast, and flow without overwhelming the viewer.

Breaking Down the Ratio

60% Dominant Color: This is your primary background or main canvas. In a room, this is the wall color. In a UI, it is the app background. This color should be neutral or subtle, providing the foundation for everything else. It sets the overall tone (e.g., a light gray for a clean app or a soft beige for a cozy site).

30% Secondary Color: This supports the dominant color. It provides contrast and visual interest. In a room, this is often the furniture or curtains. In a UI, it is used for cards, navigation bars, or large content blocks. It should harmonize with the dominant color but stand out enough to define distinct areas.

10% Accent Color: This is the "pop." It is used sparingly to draw attention to the most important elements—buttons, links, icons, or highlights. This is where you use your most vibrant or saturated colors. Because it covers the smallest area, it creates the highest impact.

Why It Works

The human brain craves balance. A 50-50 split of two colors feels static and tense. A 90-10 split feels aggressive. The 60-30-10 ratio offers a "Goldilocks" balance where the majority is calm, the minority is accentuated, and the middle ground ties it all together. It creates a visual hierarchy that guides the eye naturally.

Adapting for Digital

While interior design uses fixed rooms, digital design uses scrollable pages. The 60% rule applies to the immediate viewport or the general theme. In Dark Mode, the ratios might shift slightly (e.g., 70% dark background, 20% lighter surfaces, 10% accent), but the principle of a dominant base, a supporting theme, and a highlight accent remains valid.

Conclusion

The 60-30-10 rule is a starting point, not a law. But for designers struggling with "too many colors" or chaotic layouts, returning to this ratio is the fastest way to restore order and professionalism.

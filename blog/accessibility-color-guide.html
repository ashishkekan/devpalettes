Accessibility

Web Accessibility & Color Guide

Learn how to create inclusive designs that work for everyone.

Web accessibility ensures that websites and applications can be used by people of all abilities and disabilities. When it comes to color, accessibility primarily revolves around contrast ratios and ensuring that information is not conveyed by color alone. Designing with accessibility in mind is not just a legal requirement in many jurisdictions; it’s a moral imperative that improves the user experience for everyone.

Understanding WCAG Guidelines

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) provide specific success criteria for color contrast. For normal text, the minimum contrast ratio is 4.5:1 (Level AA), while large text requires a ratio of 3:1. For enhanced accessibility (Level AAA), the ratio increases to 7:1 for normal text. These ratios ensure that text is readable against its background for users with moderate visual impairments or color vision deficiencies.

Color Blindness Considerations

Approximately 8% of men and 0.5% of women have some form of color blindness. The most common form is red-green color blindness. Designers must ensure that they do not rely solely on color to convey meaning. For example, an error state should not just turn a border red; it should also include an icon or text label. Using our Color Blindness Simulator can help you visualize how your designs appear to users with different vision types.

Focus States and Visual Cues

Keyboard navigation relies heavily on visible focus states. When a user tabs through a page, the currently focused element must be clearly distinguishable. Many default browser focus styles have low contrast. It is best practice to design custom focus rings that meet contrast requirements (usually a 3:1 ratio against adjacent colors) to ensure keyboard users know exactly where they are on the page.

Tools for Accessible Design

Modern browsers have built-in developer tools to check contrast ratios. Additionally, standalone tools and plugins can audit your entire color palette. When selecting colors, check them against both white and black backgrounds to ensure versatility. Always aim to exceed the minimum requirements where possible to accommodate users with more severe impairments.

Conclusion

Accessible design is good design. By adhering to WCAG standards and thinking beyond color, you create interfaces that are more robust and usable in various contexts, from bright sunlight to low-light environments. Incorporate accessibility checks into your workflow early to avoid costly redesigns later.

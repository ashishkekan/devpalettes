Color Accessibility Tools

The software that ensures your design works for everyone.

Designing for accessibility isn't just a moral obligation; it's a professional standard. Fortunately, designers today have a robust toolkit to test their color choices. From ensuring text is readable to simulating color blindness, these tools bridge the gap between "it looks good to me" and "it works for everyone."

Contrast Checkers

The most fundamental tool in the arsenal. Contrast checkers (like WebAIM's Contrast Checker or Stark) take a foreground and background color and calculate the ratio. They instantly flag if text passes WCAG AA (4.5:1) or AAA (7:1) standards. Modern plugins for Figma and Sketch integrate this directly into the design workflow, allowing you to fix issues in real-time rather than retrofitting later.

Color Blindness Simulators

Tools like Coblis, Color Oracle, and the Sim Daltonism app allow designers to view their screens through the lens of different vision deficiencies (Protanopia, Deuteranopia, Tritanopia). This is critical for data visualization, where red/green contrasts might be invisible to 8% of men. Seeing your design in grayscale is a quick stress test—if it makes sense in grayscale, it will likely work for most color vision deficiencies.

Palette Generators with A11y Built-in

Tools like Coolors and Adobe Color now have "accessibility mode" or "color blind safe" filters. They generate palettes that are mathematically distinct for various vision types. Devpalettes also provides contrast scoring for generated combinations, ensuring that your random palettes are viable for production use.

Conclusion

Accessibility tools should be used early and often. They are not a final QA step but a continuous part of the design process. Incorporating them into your daily workflow ensures that accessibility becomes a habit, not a hurdle.

UI Components

Card Design & Color

Creating hierarchy and focus in container components.

The "Card" is one of the most ubiquitous UI components. It is a flexible container used to group related information. From product listings to blog excerpts, cards organize content into digestible chunks. Color is the primary tool for defining a card's boundaries and its relationship to the background.

Elevation and Shadow

In Material Design, cards float. They use shadows to suggest elevation. A higher card casts a larger shadow, appearing closer to the user. In light mode, the card is white against a gray background. In dark mode, the logic flips. A light shadow on a dark card doesn't make sense; instead, a darker gray card is used against a black background. The "elevation" is communicated through luminosity, not shadow.

Bordered vs Shadowed Cards

Modern minimalism has shifted away from heavy shadows towards thin borders. A bordered card sits flat against the page, feeling more modern and clean. This approach works exceptionally well in dark mode, where shadows are less visible. The choice depends on the aesthetic: use shadows for a tactile, app-like feel; use borders for a sleek, editorial look.

Interactive States

If a card is clickable, it needs interactive states. On hover, the card should respond—either lifting (translateY), glowing (box-shadow), or border-highlighting. This feedback is crucial for discoverability. A static, flat card might be mistaken for a background element.

Conclusion

Cards are the building blocks of modern layouts. Their color and depth treatment sets the tone for the entire interface.
